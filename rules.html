What's Allowed, What Kills Accounts, What Killed Companies

The single most important finding of this whole pack: astrology consulting is not categorically banned anywhere in our stack for US/CA/UK/FR/EU — not on Google, Meta, Microsoft, or Stripe. Every dead company in this niche died from the same three self-inflicted wounds: deceptive free-reading funnels, guaranteed-outcome claims, and billing tricks. All policy pages verified live 2026-07-13.

Google Ads — allowed, with discretionary risk

  • Not named anywhere. Live fetch of the full Ads policy hub (2026-07-13): zero mentions of astrology, psychic, or horoscope across all prohibited and restricted categories. Verified 3-0.
  • The discretionary clause. Under "Other restricted businesses" Google reserves the right to limit or stop ads from entire business categories it deems risky, "even if they comply with other policies." Eligibility is therefore never guaranteed — this is the background risk, not an active ban.
  • The real enforcement path: Misrepresentation. The Misrepresentation policy and its "Unreliable claims" sub-policy ban improbable-result enticements, miracle cures, and unrealistic promises of financial or personal outcomes. Any "guaranteed results" language requires a clearly displayed money-back policy.
  • Escalation ladder. Ad disapproval first → 3-strike system → permanent account suspension for repeated or egregious violations. Related accounts sharing a payment method get suspended together — which means one burned astrology account can take down every other ads account on the same card/profile.
Copy rule for every Google ad we ever run: no promised outcomes ("get your ex back", "attract wealth"), no cure language, no guarantees without a money-back policy displayed on the landing page. Sell the reading, the expertise, and the experience — never the result.

Sources (all verified 3-0, fetched live 2026-07-13): Ads policy hub · Misrepresentation · Unreliable claims · Account suspension

Meta (Facebook/Instagram) — allowed, but the Personal Attributes trap sits exactly on our angles

Meta permits promoting astrology services. The trap: the Personal Attributes policy prohibits any ad copy that asserts or implies knowledge of the viewer's personal characteristics — explicitly including "beliefs", "religion", "physical or mental health", and "vulnerable financial status". Those are precisely the four angles (love anxiety, career, money, health) this business sells into. Verified 3-0 against the live policy.

✅ Passes review

  • "Get your full natal chart read by a certified astrologer"
  • "Book a career-timing consultation"
  • "Compatibility readings for couples — 60 minutes, live"
  • Meta's own allowed example: "Date Christian singles!" — third-person, no implied knowledge

⛔ Gets disapproved (and repeat offenses burn the account)

  • "Anxious about your marriage?" — implies knowledge of mental state
  • "Are you broke?" / "Struggling financially?" — vulnerable financial status
  • "If you've been diagnosed with anxiety…" — health attribute
  • Meta's own violating example: "Are you Christian?" — second-person belief targeting
The compliant formula: describe the service and who it's for in third person ("readings for people navigating career changes"), never the viewer's situation in second person ("stuck in your career?").

Source (verified 3-0, fetched live 2026-07-13): Meta Personal Attributes policy

Microsoft Ads — the quiet green lane

  • Restricted in ~105 countries — none of ours. The "Psychics, Tarot Reading, and Mediums" restricted-content list does not include the US, CA, UK, FR, DE, IT, ES, or NL — verified against the Apr 2024 policy snapshot (105 counted; an earlier extraction counted 106; the policy URL has since moved into Microsoft's help hub, so recount at launch).
  • Conditions apply everywhere: all claims must be presented as entertainment, lifestyle, or spiritual guidance; no medical claims, no guaranteed outcomes, no fear-based messaging.
  • Geo-targeting watch-outs: Romania (an EU member) IS on the restricted list; so are 5 US territories (incl. Puerto Rico and Guam) plus Aruba, Greenland, Faroe Islands. Broad "EU" or "US + territories" targeting needs explicit exclusions.
  • Refuted claim we will NOT act on: the claim that non-search formats (Display/Native/Audience Network) are banned for this category in permitted countries failed verification 0-3. All formats are available in target geos, subject to the same content rules.

Sources (verified 3-0): Microsoft restricted-category policy · policy help

Stripe & payments — usable, not trustable

  • Not on our lists. Live fetch of Stripe's Prohibited and Restricted Businesses list (2026-07-13), two targeted passes: no astrology/psychic/tarot/horoscope/occult terms in the global section or the US, CA, UK, FR, EU country sections. "Psychic services and fortune tellers" appears only under Japan, Mexico, and Thailand. Verified 3-0.
  • Stripe removed "psychic services" from its global prohibited list in late 2021 — the category went from banned to jurisdiction-specific.
  • Restricted ≠ impossible anyway: Stripe's own FAQ: "Stripe might be able to support businesses on its Prohibited and Restricted Businesses list with explicit prior approval."
  • The de-facto reality: high-risk by underwriting. Multiple documented practitioner reports (3amtarot, tarotandchai, a change.org petition, payment-industry coverage) show Stripe and its banking partners closing US tarot/psychic/energy-work accounts at their discretion despite the category's absence from the list. Absence from the list is not safety.
Non-negotiable architecture decision: Stripe as primary processor + a high-risk-friendly backup (dedicated psychic/tarot merchant-account providers exist as a documented industry) signed up from day one, with the checkout able to fail over. A frozen Stripe account with 90-day fund holds mid-launch is the single most likely infrastructure catastrophe in this niche.

Sources (verified 3-0): Stripe restricted businesses · Stripe FAQ · considerations

The enforcement graveyard — what actually killed astrology/psychic businesses

🇺🇸 Miss Cleo / Access Resource Services — FTC, November 2002

  • The damage: forgave an estimated $500,000,000 in outstanding consumer charges and paid $5M to settle FTC charges. Verified 3-0 against the FTC's own press release.
  • The trigger: the exact funnel this niche still loves — "free psychic reading" ads that converted into per-minute billing, misrepresenting that readings were free and misrepresenting per-call costs.
  • The kill shot was injunctive, not monetary: the stipulated order required ceasing ALL pay-per-call activity, banned solicitation calls without opt-out, and prohibited any material misrepresentation — the FTC shut the business model down entirely, not just fined it.
  • Still live law: conduct injunctions remain a fully available FTC remedy today (AMG Capital 2021 limited only monetary relief).

Source: FTC press release, Nov 2002 (verified 3-0)

🇺🇸 International psychic mail-fraud scheme — US DOJ (consent decree)

The Department of Justice permanently shut down an international psychic mail-fraud operation (the Maria Duval-linked scheme) that mailed personalized "psychic" solicitations promising wealth to millions of elderly and vulnerable consumers. The operational lesson is identical to Miss Cleo: personalized supernatural promises of money/luck to vulnerable people is prosecutable fraud, regardless of the astrology/psychic label on top.

Source: DOJ press release

Pattern across every dead company: none died for being astrology businesses. They died for (1) fake-free offers, (2) billing that didn't match what was promised, (3) preying on vulnerability with outcome promises. All three are design choices we simply don't make.

UK — the strictest ad-copy regime in our geo set

  • CAP guidance explicitly covers astrologers ("Marketing by spiritualists, clairvoyants, psychics, fortune tellers, astrologers etc"). Ads must NOT claim to: solve all problems, break curses, banish evil spirits, or improve health, wealth, love life or happiness — these are treated as unprovable claims. Ads must not exploit vulnerable people. Verified 3-0 against the CAP guidance PDF.
  • CAP Code rule 3.7 — evidence BEFORE publication. The marketer must hold documentary evidence for any objectively substantiable claim (reading accuracy, "guaranteed" results, "celebrity adviser", "as featured on TV") before the ad runs, or the ASA can rule it misleading. Enforcement example on record: an unsubstantiated "world renowned" claim.
  • Refuted framing we will NOT repeat: the common blog claim that UK psychics are "regulated under the 2008 CPRs with a reversed burden of proof" failed verification 1-2 as stated. The operative UK regime for our purposes is the CAP Code + ASA rulings above.
UK copy rule: UK ads describe the service, the astrologer's credentials (provable), session length and price. Nothing about outcomes, wellbeing, love, or money — and every factual claim sits in an evidence folder before the campaign goes live.

Sources (verified 3-0): ASA/CAP spiritual & psychic services guidance (updated 31 Mar 2026) · CAP guidance PDF · CAP 3.7 substantiation

Consumer law & data protection, jurisdiction by jurisdiction

Dedicated legal-verification pass (2026-07-14), primary sources: Légifrance, EUR-Lex, legislation.gov.uk, CNIL, FTC, court opinions. Headline: selling astrology is legal per se in all five jurisdictions — France abolished its fortune-telling offence in 1994, Canada repealed s.365 in 2018, the UK regulates it as ordinary trading since 2008, US bans are largely unconstitutional. The live risk everywhere is consumer/fraud/privacy law.

🇫🇷 France — legal since 1 Mar 1994, but two sharp edges

  • The old ban is gone. Ancien Code pénal Art. R.34, 7° (the divination contravention) was abrogated by Décret n° 93-726, effective 1 Mar 1994 with the new Penal Code, and never re-enacted. No licence or qualification required — ordinary business registration, standard 20% VAT. (Légifrance, primary)
  • Misleading commercial practices: Code de la consommation Arts. L.121-2 ff. — false claims about the expected results of a service are criminal: up to 2 years + €300,000, raisable to 10% of average annual turnover. Promising concrete outcomes ("your ex returns", "your finances will recover") is prosecutable — services must be framed as interpretive guidance.
  • 14-day withdrawal right (Art. L.221-18) on distance services. To deliver a reading inside 14 days without refund exposure: explicit "start now + I acknowledge losing my withdrawal right on full performance" checkbox before delivery (Art. L.221-28 1°/13°).
  • Live DGCCRF enforcement: Nov 2024 — two Marseille voyance companies convicted of misleading and aggressive practices (premium-rate solicitations, broken unsubscribe); over €2M in assets confiscated. The sector is actively watched.
  • Abus de faiblesse — the French killer clause. Penal Code Art. 223-15-2: fraudulently exploiting a person's ignorance or weakness (age, illness, distress, psychological subjection) = 3 years + €375,000 — raised to 5 years + €750,000 when committed online (2024 anti-cult-abuses law). This is the standard charge against predatory psychics; a fortune-teller who extracted ~€200K from an elderly client got 2 years + €50K (Paris CA 2022, single-source). Operationally: hard spend-caps and refund paths for repeat high-spend distressed customers are a legal-defense feature, not generosity.
  • Fraud case law is actually favorable: selling divination to a willing buyer is NOT automatically escroquerie — conviction requires staged fraudulent manoeuvres (Cass. crim. 19 Feb 2013, n° 12-80.440).

🇪🇺 GDPR — the sharpest edge in the whole legal file

  • The controlling precedent is directly on point. CNIL, 26 Sep 2024: online voyance platforms Cosmospace fined €250,000 and Télémaque €150,000. Holdings: consultation content where customers disclose love/sex life, health, or religious beliefs IS Article 9 special-category data requiring explicit consent — and "merely being willing to receive divination services and spontaneously providing sensitive information cannot be regarded as explicit consent." Also sanctioned: recording all calls (data minimisation) and 6-year marketing retention (max ~3 years recommended).
  • What this means for us: a granular, prior, sensitive-data consent checkbox in the booking flow; no blanket call recording; ≤3-year marketing retention; documented consent logs. Birth date/time/place by itself is ordinary personal data — the readings' content is what triggers Art. 9.
  • Non-EU company serving EU customers → Art. 27 EU representative (and a UK representative under UK GDPR Art. 27). The exemption for "occasional" processing is unavailable to a business doing regular sensitive-data readings at scale. Cookie consent per CNIL/ICO; DPIA once processing is large-scale.
  • Open point (no authority either way): whether a customer's belief in astrology is itself an Art. 9 "philosophical belief" — prudent posture is to treat subscriber lists as potentially Art. 9 and rely on explicit consent, which the CNIL decision forces anyway.

Source: CNIL sanction decision, Sep 2024 (primary regulator decision)

🇬🇧 UK consumer law — updated regime as of 6 Apr 2025

  • History: Fraudulent Mediums Act 1951 repealed 26 May 2008 by the CPUTR 2008 — psychics/astrologers became ordinary "traders". CPUTR itself was repealed and replaced from 6 Apr 2025 by the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (Part 4 Ch 1).
  • The DMCCA Schedule 20 blacklist (banned in all circumstances): para 18 — claiming products can facilitate winning in games of chance; para 19 — falsely claiming a product can prevent/treat disease or modify physiological function. The CMA can now directly fine up to 10% of global turnover.
  • Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013: 14-day cancellation on distance services; right lost on full performance only with the customer's express "start now" request + acknowledgment (reg. 36(4), reg. 37 for digital content). Missing the model cancellation information extends the window up to 12 months.

Sources: DMCCA 2024 Sch. 20 · CCRs 2013 (primary)

🇪🇺 EU-wide rules

  • UCPD Annex I (identical blacklist in every member state): item 16 — claiming products can facilitate winning in games of chance (no falsity qualifier — banned outright, so "this reading helps you win the lottery" is illegal everywhere in the EU even if you believe it); item 17 — falsely claiming a product cures illness.
  • Omnibus Directive price rules: any "€99 → €29 launch offer" must state the prior price as the lowest price of the preceding 30 days; algorithmically personalised prices must be disclosed. Fines up to at least 4% of turnover for widespread infringements.
  • VAT: automated products (instant chart PDFs, app content) = electronically supplied services taxed in the customer's country via OSS. Live 1:1 human consultations are NOT e-services (supplier-location rule) — but whether the post-2025 virtual-events rule captures 1:1 consultations is unsettled; needs a VAT adviser opinion at scale.

Sources: UCPD consolidated text (primary) · Bird & Bird / Deloitte VAT analyses

🇨🇦 Canada

  • The fortune-telling offence is gone: Criminal Code s. 365 repealed 13 Dec 2018 (Bill C-51). Ordinary fraud (s. 380) still covers deceptive extraction of money.
  • Competition Act s. 74.01: no materially false/misleading representations, judged by "general impression"; performance claims need adequate substantiation.
  • CASL is the strictest email law in our geo set: express/implied consent + sender ID with physical mailing address + unsubscribe honoured within 10 business days. Penalties to $1M (individuals) / $10M (organisations). No purchased lists, no pre-checked boxes, log every consent.

🇺🇸 US — patchwork, mostly toothless for online, with three named exceptions

  • No federal ban. First Amendment case law strikes down outright bans (Argello v. City of Lincoln, 8th Cir. 1998; Nefedro v. Montgomery County, Md. 2010) but neutral licence/permit regimes survive (Moore-King v. County of Chesterfield, 4th Cir. 2013).
  • Surviving state criminal statutes: NY Penal Law § 165.35 (class B misdemeanor to claim occult powers for a fee — with an express exemption for entertainment shows), and similar retained offences in Pennsylvania (18 Pa.C.S. § 7104) and Oklahoma. Massachusetts requires municipal licensing; Virginia allows a local licence tax.
  • Astrology is NOT legally distinct from "fortune telling" — where statutes define the term, they enumerate astrology within it.
  • Online application: absence of authority. No reported case applies a municipal fortune-telling licence or state ban to a purely online/out-of-state service — not a safe harbor, but the real-world US exposure is FTC Act §5 deception + ROSCA subscription rules + state UDAP, not fortune-telling statutes.

Sources: NY Penal Law § 165.35 · Argello · Nefedro · Moore-King (primary opinions)

The "entertainment purposes only" disclaimer — verified status

Verified finding (two independent passes): the "entertainment purposes only" disclaimer is not legally required by any federal law in any target geo. Its only real statutory footing is that it maps onto express exemptions like NY Penal Law § 165.35 ("show or exhibition solely for the purpose of entertainment or amusement"). What it does: (a) may bring you inside such an exemption, (b) gives an argument against deception claims. What it does NOT do: cure a misleading net impression — under FTC doctrine a disclaimer never immunises deceptive claims, and the NY exemption requires the conduct be solely entertainment, which selling "real" guidance undercuts. Treatment in the roadmap: a professionally-worded "guidance — not a substitute for professional medical, legal or financial advice" disclaimer on the site and in terms (the honest formulation that also protects a premium consulting brand), plus Keen-style 18+ age restriction — the disclaimer standard large incumbents actually use.
