Certification — what it does and doesn't do

  • Major sellers ship third-party lab certificates with every stone (IGI, GIA, GRS, GII, IGI-GTL); GII is a project of GJEPC, India's government-sponsored export council
  • Certificates attest identity and treatment only (natural vs heated/glass-filled) — never astrological efficacy
  • Documented standard fraud vector in astro gems: concealed heat/glass-filling treatment

The remedy-selling rulebook — documented enforcement

🇬🇧 UK — the hardest line (ASA rulings on record)

  • Spirit Walker Crystals (2013): site implied crystals help heal Alzheimer's, asthma, depression, heart conditions — ruled misleading, no clinical evidence
  • Octopus Publishing (2005): even a GENERIC reference to crystals' "healing power" ruled misleading absent clinical evidence
  • CAP rule 12.1: treatment claims need clinical-trial-grade evidence; discouraging essential medical care = irresponsible advertising. ASA still enforcing this hard line into 2025–26

🇺🇸 US + 🇮🇳 India

  • FTC COVID sweep (2020, ~300 warning letters) explicitly named "gemstone bead bracelets" among fake-treatment pitches — claims to stop within 48h on pain of injunction + refunds
  • No FTC case specifically against an astrology-gem remedy seller found; no landmark Indian consumer-commission ruling found either (genuine gap — remedy claims are effectively unregulated in India, fraud route is IPC 420/consumer forum)
The operating rule this dictates: gemstones are sold as jewelry and spiritual tradition — "recommended for your chart per Vedic tradition" — never as treatment for health, and never with wealth/health outcome promises. Every product page carries authenticity certification; no efficacy claims anywhere. This is the compliant version of AstroTalk's consultation→remedy funnel.

Sources: ASA/CAP crystal therapy guidance · FTC 2020 sweep

The billing-model risk file (this is where the niche gets hurt)

  • FTC Click-to-Cancel rule: vacated, not dead. The Negative Option Rule was struck down by the Eighth Circuit on 8 Jul 2025 on procedural grounds; the FTC restarted rulemaking 30 Jan 2026 (comments closed 13 Apr 2026). Meanwhile ROSCA enforcement continues and state auto-renewal laws (California et al.) fully apply. Trial-to-subscription funnels remain actionable today.
  • Nebula = the cautionary benchmark — and the FTC agrees. ~1,400+ overwhelmingly negative Trustpilot reviews on its web funnel: $0.99 "report" trials converting to unauthorized $24.99–£49.99 recurring charges, web-billed subs invisible in Apple's subscription manager, failed cancellations, billing after cancellation. BBB Scam Tracker entries on record. Then, on 2 Jun 2026, the FTC sued Nebula's parent enterprise (Genesis Tech — 15 corporations, 8 individuals) for ROSCA/FTC-Act violations, and a federal court temporarily halted parts of its US operations (court records: >$37.4M taken from US consumers via PayPal alone, Jan 2023–Jun 2025). Full detail on Giants.
  • Design consequence: transparent pricing at checkout, email receipts before every renewal, one-click cancellation, no dark-pattern trials. This isn't just ethics — with Stripe already treating the vertical as fragile (see Rules), a Nebula-style chargeback profile is how the account dies.

Sources: Sidley · Gibson Dunn · Trustpilot: appnebula.co

Lifetime-deal (LTD) precedents — what exists, what doesn't

The in-niche precedent

  • Moonly (moon-calendar/astrology app, 6M+ downloads): Lifetime Pro $39.99 one-time, while current Annual Pro is $59.99 (intro year $39.99) — the lifetime unlock now sits at or below one year's price (live App Store check 2026-07-14; the earlier $29.99/yr price made it 1.3× annual)
  • Co-Star: $2.99–11.99 one-time permanent report unlocks — one-time-purchase precedent in-niche
  • Verified absence: NO major astrology app (CHANI, Co-Star, Pattern, Nebula) has ever run an LTD on AppSumo or StackSocial — searched both platforms

Deal-platform norms (adjacent evidence)

  • AppSumo LTDs: $29–299 one-time, average ~$69–99, positioned at 50–95% off subscription equivalent; buyer break-even typically 2–5 months of the sub price
  • StackSocial: $9–99 typical, $299+ premium tier
What this means for the planned 100–200-seat LTD: the precedents price lifetime CONTENT cheap — Moonly's lifetime now sits at or below its own annual price, and AppSumo LTDs average $69–99 — far too cheap a format to attach unlimited human consultations to. The precedent supports a lifetime software/content tier, not lifetime 1:1 service. The Roadmap therefore structures the founding-member LTD as: lifetime content + a fixed, finite consultation allocation (not unlimited), 100–200 seats, priced as a multiple of the annual tier — the only version of the idea the niche's economics support.

Sources: Moonly App Store · 99signals AppSumo data · StackSocial
