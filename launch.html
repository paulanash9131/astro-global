The whole research, on one page

If you read nothing else, read these six things. Each one is backed by real sources in the research pack. Here is what they mean for us, in plain words.

✅ It is legal. In every country we want.

Google, Facebook, Microsoft and Stripe all allow astrology in America, the UK, Canada and Europe. France removed its old law against fortune telling in 1994. Canada removed its one in 2018. In America, courts have thrown most of these old laws out. So there is no licence to buy and nobody to ask for permission. The only rules are about how we word our ads and how we charge people — never about whether we are allowed to do this.

⚠️ Lots of people like astrology — but far fewer will PAY

Yes, 78 million American adults look at astrology each year. But most of them do it just for fun and will never pay for a reading. The people who really pay are only 6 to 10 out of every 100 adults — about 16 to 26 million people. That is still a lot. And we know exactly who they are: women aged 18 to 49, younger adults, and Indian families living abroad who want Vedic readings. We sell good readings to those people. We do not chase everyone.

💰 The money is in real people talking, not apps

Every big winner sells time with a real human. AstroTalk in India made about $140 million last year, and has made a profit every single year, just by charging per minute for readings. Kasamba makes about $37 million a year. Meanwhile the famous astrology apps make far less. One app called Sanctuary started with no humans — and had to add live readings just to survive. So your partner doing the readings is not a weakness. It IS the business.

🚨 Every company that got destroyed did the same 3 things

They offered a "free reading" and then charged people. They promised results. And they made it hard to cancel. Miss Cleo was forced to cancel $500 million of customer bills. A French company was fined €250,000 for how it handled customer data. And last month the US government sued Nebula's owner and a court stopped part of its business — for exactly this. We simply do not do any of those three things. It costs us nothing, and it is the whole difference between a real business and a court case.

🧮 The limit: one astrologer can only earn about $26,000 a month

Six readings a day, five days a week, at $200 each. That is the most one person can ever do. Every serious company hits this wall. So from the start we also sell recorded readings (he records them alone, so there is no limit), monthly plans, and a one-time founder offer. That way the business keeps earning even when his calendar is full.

💎 Gemstones make money — but only if he suggests them in a reading

AstroTalk's stone shop went from zero to about $24 million a year in just 14 months — selling small items, around $10 each. Meanwhile normal crystal shops in the West only make $2 to $5 million a year. Why the huge difference? Because the astrologer tells the client which stone they need during a paid reading, and then the shop sends it. So we cannot start the shop first. We need readings happening first. That is why stones come last.

One honest warning about the 50/50 deal: in a good month 6 to 9, we might earn $25,000 to $35,000 and spend $10,000 to $12,000. That is real money — but split in half, it is not life-changing yet. The reason to give away half the company is what comes after: the stone shop, and adding more astrologers so the business is not stuck with one man's calendar. The partnership paper must allow that from day one. If we ask later, when it is finally working, he can simply say no.

1.4 · The legal pack Required I write these

These are not decoration. The people who approve us at Stripe, Google and Facebook all read these pages. If they are missing, we get rejected before we ever get one client.

  • We need these pages on our website: rules of use, privacy promise, refund and cancel rules, cookie notice, and a contact page with a real email that someone actually reads.
  • Our safety line, worded well: "This is guidance. It is not a replacement for a doctor, a lawyer or a financial adviser." We also say clients must be 18 or older. Note: the famous line "for entertainment purposes only" is NOT required by law anywhere. It does not protect us, and it makes us look cheap. We use the honest line instead.
  • The GDPR consent architecture (this is the CNIL Cosmospace lesson — they were fined €250,000 for exactly this): an explicit, separate, unticked checkbox in the booking flow covering the sensitive things a reading touches. Not buried in the T&Cs.
  • A tick-box for UK and European clients. By law they can cancel and get a refund within 14 days. So before the reading, they must tick: "Start my reading now. I understand that once it is done, I cannot ask for my money back." Without this box, anyone in Europe can take the full reading and still demand a refund.
  • Never write a fake review. Never pay for one. Since April 2025, UK law can fine a company 10% of everything it earns worldwide for fake reviews, and they started punishing companies in March 2026. Every review we show is from a real client who gave us written permission.

1.5 · Call recordings — get this exactly right Legal landmine

We record every reading, because the client wants the recording — it is part of what they paid for. But recording someone without asking properly is a CRIME in parts of America and against the law in Europe. In California alone, a client can sue for $5,000 per recording, even if they were not harmed at all.

  • Two empty tick-boxes when they book: one saying "yes, record me", and one saying "yes, I know we may talk about private things like my health, my relationships and my beliefs".
  • In the first 20 seconds of every call, he asks out loud for permission — while the recording is already running — and waits for the client to say "yes" out loud. This one small habit keeps us legal in the strict US states, in California, in Canada and in Europe, all at once. I will write him the exact words to say.
  • If the client says no: stop the recording immediately, say out loud that you have stopped, and do the reading without recording. This must really work — Canadian law requires us to offer this choice.
  • The little Zoom "recording" message is NOT enough. Neither is a beep sound. A court in California has never accepted those, and they would not protect us.
  • Keep recordings in a proper business account, locked, and delete them automatically after 12 months. Never keep them in someone's personal Google Drive.

Launch & first customers

Weeks 8–12 · the reality check
Read this before spending a single rupee on ads. The big astrology companies PAY other people $75 to $175 to bring them one new paying client. But buying that same client yourself on Google costs about $109 to $182. Look at those two numbers: ads barely break even in this business. That is exactly why the big companies pay other people instead of buying ads themselves. So we do NOT start by throwing money at ads and hoping. We start with the one thing nobody else has: our astrologer himself. Ads come later, and only in small amounts, and only if the numbers work.

3.1 · Warm launch — the first 20 clients come free I run this

Your partner already reads for clients in the UK and France. Those people already trust him. Getting them back costs us nothing.

  • Send a personal email and WhatsApp to every single person he has ever read for: tell them about the new business, send the booking link, and give them a real thank-you discount (a real one, never a fake one).
  • Ask every happy client for a written review and written permission to show it. Photo and full name are optional. Goal: 10 reviews before we spend anything on ads. Rule: reviews must describe what the reading was like, never promise a result. UK regulators would punish us for the second kind.
  • Ask each of them to tell one friend. For every single small astrologer we studied, friends telling friends was the biggest source of clients.
  • Set up a simple referral reward: give a friend $25 off, get $25 credit.
  • The goal here: our first 15 to 25 paying clients without spending anything on ads, plus enough reviews to make the website look trustworthy.

3.2 · Organic content — the channel that actually built this niche He fronts it I produce it

Every small astrologer we found who got past $10,000 a month did it with videos, not ads. One of them (Maren Altman) built a TikTok following and now charges $200 for a 15-minute reading and $1,500 for a course. This is slow and free, and it grows on its own — so we start in week 8, not month 8.

  • What the videos are: your partner on camera for 45 to 90 seconds, answering ONE real question. No fancy filming needed. People just need to see his face and hear that he knows his subject.
  • How often: 4 or 5 short videos a week, posted to TikTok, Instagram and YouTube at the same time. He records 10 in one sitting. The helper and I do the cutting, the captions and the posting.
  • What to post: 7 out of 10 videos should simply teach something (this builds trust). 2 should show proof (real client stories, names hidden, with permission). Only 1 should actually sell anything.
  • Every video ends the same way: "Get your free birth chart at [our website]". The videos collect emails. The emails sell the readings. We never sell hard inside the video itself.
  • The free birth chart is our bait. A website called astro.com has used this exact trick for 40 years: give away a really good free chart, then sell the deeper reading. Nothing in this business works better.
  • Turn every video topic into a blog post too, so Google finds us. A site called Cafe Astrology gets 4.5 to 6 million visits a month doing only this — and 7 out of 10 of its readers are women aged 25 to 34. That is exactly who we want.

3.3 · Paid ads — small, surgical, and only where the maths works I run this $60–100/day to start

Because ads barely break even, we start small and prove one channel works before spending more. We are not paying to reach people who are curious about astrology. We are paying to reach people who are ready to BUY a reading.

  • Google Search only, to start. We only pay for people searching to BUY: "vedic astrologer consultation", "birth chart reading online", "astrology consultation near me". We never pay for the word "horoscope" or "daily horoscope" — those people are just having fun and they never buy.
  • What a click really costs: cheap words are worthless, and buying words cost 10 to 40 times more. Expect to pay about $5 to $7 for one click. Ignore the free keyword tools showing 30 cents — those numbers are averages from India and they will mislead you.
  • Copy whatever works into Microsoft/Bing ads too. Allowed in all our countries, clicks are cheaper, and the people are older and richer. One warning: turn OFF Romania and the small US islands — astrology ads are restricted there.
  • Facebook and Instagram come second, only after something is already selling. And the wording must never suggest we know anything about the person — not their health, not their beliefs, not their money problems. Facebook bans that instantly.
  • TikTok ads are allowed for astrology in America, the UK and Canada — I checked their rulebook. But posting on TikTok for free works better anyway, so that comes first.
  • Rule for every single ad, forever: never promise a result, never say "100% accurate", never scare people, never say "free" when it is not free. This is what destroyed Miss Cleo, and it is what gets ad accounts banned for life.
  • Keep the card separate: the ad account uses one card, used for nothing else ever. If Google bans one account, it also bans every other account that shares the same card. One mistake could wipe out all our accounts at once.

🚦 The Day-90 Gate — we do not scale until these are true

  • Cost per booked session ≤ 40% of first-order value — i.e. under ~$80 on a $195 session. If ads can't hit this, we stop ads and double down on organic + referral.
  • ≥ 25% of month-one clients book a follow-up or subscribe within 60 days. Below that, the product isn't retaining and more traffic just wastes money.
  • Refund rate ≤ 5% and card dispute rate ≤ 0.4% — above that, the payment processor becomes the existential risk, not growth.
  • ≥ 10 published testimonials and a repeatable content cadence running.

If we fail two of these four, we STOP and fix things before spending another dollar. This check is what separates a real business from an expensive hobby.

4.2 · Billing that won't get us killed Non-negotiable Card-network law

This is where astrology companies get killed. In June 2026 the US government sued Nebula's owner over exactly this. Visa's own rulebook says exactly what we must do — and doing it costs us nothing.

  • At least 7 days before we charge them again, or before any price change: send a reminder email. Also required.

  • Send a reminder before EVERY charge, not just the first. Not required everywhere, but it is the cheapest way to stop people complaining to their bank.
  • The name on their bank statement must be our brand name, the one they recognise. "I don't know what this charge is" is the number one reason people complain to their bank.
  • Switch on the free Ethoca tool. When a client taps the charge in their banking app, it shows our name, logo and what they bought — so they remember, and they do not complain. Free, and the single best thing we can turn on.
  • No free trials that quietly turn into paid. We sell an honest, clearly-priced first reading instead. This one decision removes the exact risk that destroyed Miss Cleo and got Nebula sued.

The gemstone arm

Months 6–12 · only after consultation volume exists
Why this is last, not first. AstroTalk's remedies store went from zero to ₹200 Cr ARR (~$24M) in 14 months — but it works because the astrologer prescribes the remedy inside a paid consultation and the store fulfils it. Standalone Western crystal shops, by contrast, top out at $2–5M/year even for the biggest names. The commerce rides on the trust. No consultations, no gemstone business.

5.1 · How we build it I build

  • Months 6–8: curated dropship. The astrologer recommends a stone in a Vedic consultation; the recommendation and an order link go into the post-session summary. No inventory risk.
  • Months 9–12: once we know which 10 SKUs actually sell, buy inventory for those and run our own store on a subdomain.
  • Every stone ships with a third-party lab certificate (IGI/GIA-grade). This is the industry norm among serious sellers — and the fraud in this category is concealed heat/glass-filling treatment, so certification is the trust differentiator, not a nice-to-have.
  • Declare it to the bank and processor before launching — Airwallex classes loose gemstones as a restricted category needing approval. Adding it quietly is how accounts get frozen.
  • Separate merchant descriptor for the store, so a dispute spike on physical goods never threatens the consulting rail.

5.2 · The claims rule — this is what keeps us out of court Absolute

Gemstones are sold as jewellery and Vedic tradition — "recommended for your chart per Jyotish tradition." Never as treatment for anything. The record is unambiguous: the UK regulator ruled against a crystal seller for implying stones help heal Alzheimer's, asthma and depression, and ruled that even a generic "healing power" claim is misleading without clinical evidence. The FTC named "gemstone bead bracelets" in its fake-treatment warning sweep. And under UK law, claiming a product can "prevent or treat disease… or any adverse condition of body or mind" is banned outright — which catches "this stone will ease your anxiety." No health claims. No wealth guarantees. No luck promises. Ever.

The compliant-copy card

Print this. Every ad, every email, every page follows it. I pulled real, currently-running astrology ads from Meta's Ad Library to see what actually survives review — and the pattern is consistent and learnable.

✅ This is what works (from live, running ads)

  • Describe the system in the third person: "In astrology, your rising sign represents the mask you wear in the world."
  • Ask about a situation, not an attribute: "Navigating a career decision?" ✅ — never "Feeling anxious?" ❌
  • Let the quiz do the personalising. The ad never claims to know anything about them; they hand over their birth details after the click. This is the core mechanic of the entire niche.
  • Soft, hedged language: clarity, guidance, insight, direction. "You could have clarity."
  • Import trust instead of asserting it: real credentials, real review counts, real press.
  • Sell the service, the duration, the price, the credential.

⛔ This is what gets you banned or sued

  • Never name their sign at you ("As a Scorpio, you've been betrayed"). Not one live ad I found does this — it's the tripwire that's actually automated.
  • Never imply you know their health, money, faith or mental state. "Struggling financially?" "Anxious about your marriage?" — instant Meta rejection.
  • No guaranteed outcomes. No "bring your ex back," no "100% accurate," no "manifest $10k," no curse removal.
  • No health or healing framing at all. Not "heal your trauma," not "cure your anxiety."
  • No lottery/gambling luck claims — banned outright across the entire EU and UK, no exceptions, no defence.
  • No fake urgency, no fake scarcity, no fake reviews — all now banned practices in the UK with 10%-of-turnover fines.
  • No "free reading" that turns into a bill. The single most dangerous sentence in this industry.
